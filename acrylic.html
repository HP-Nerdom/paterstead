Material Overview

Why Acrylic

Acrylic (also sold as Plexiglas, Lucite, or PMMA) is the go-to for DIY transparent enclosures. It's lighter than glass, easier to cut without special tools, and solvent-welds into a bond that's essentially permanent — the two pieces fuse into one material rather than just sticking together. The result is watertight, strong, and clear.

Setup

Materials & Tools

Acrylic Sheet

  • Thickness: ¼" (6mm) is the standard for most enclosures — rigid enough to hold shape, light enough to work with. Use 3/16" for small enclosures (under 12"). Use 3/8" or ½" for large aquariums or anything holding significant water weight.
  • Cell-cast vs. extruded: Cell-cast acrylic is higher quality, bonds better with solvent, and is easier to machine cleanly. Extruded is cheaper but can gum up saw blades and doesn't solvent-weld as reliably. Cell-cast is worth the extra cost for anything water-holding.
  • Where to buy: Home Depot and Lowe's carry it in limited sizes. Tap Plastics, US Plastics, or local plastics suppliers stock full sheets and will often cut to size for a small fee — saves a lot of hassle.
  • Leave the masking on until the last possible step. Acrylic comes with paper or film masking that protects from scratches — keep it on through all cutting and drilling. Only peel when ready to final-assemble or display.

Joining: Solvent Cement

  • Weld-On #3 or #4: The standard for acrylic. Very thin, water-like consistency. Applied with a needle applicator bottle — capillary action draws it into the joint. #3 sets faster; #4 has slightly more working time and is more forgiving for beginners.
  • Weld-On #16: Thicker, paste-like. Better for filling gaps or joining pieces that don't fit perfectly flush. Slower cure.
  • Not silicone: Silicone doesn't bond acrylic — it just sits on the surface. Solvent cement is what creates the actual molecular bond. Silicone can be used as a secondary seal on a finished joint but not as the primary adhesive.

Cutting Tools

  • Score-and-snap: Works for straight cuts on thinner acrylic (up to ¼"). Score deeply with an acrylic scoring tool or utility knife — 5–10 passes — then snap over a straight edge. Clean, fast, no power tools needed.
  • Table saw: Best for precise straight cuts on thick stock. Use a fine-tooth blade (60–80 tooth) designed for plastics or non-ferrous metals. Go slow to prevent melting and chipping.
  • Circular saw or jigsaw: Works but harder to control. Use a fine-tooth blade and masking tape on the cut line to reduce chipping.
  • Router: Excellent for cleaning up edges or making dadoes/rabbets for lid tracks. Spiral upcut bit recommended.
  • No hacksaw or coarse blades: Coarse teeth chip and crack acrylic edges badly.

Other Tools

  • Needle applicator bottles (for Weld-On) — these come with the solvent or can be bought separately
  • Right-angle clamps or masking tape — hold pieces square while cement sets
  • Speed square or combination square — check for square before cementing
  • Sandpaper (220–400 grit) — smooth cut edges before joining
  • Flame polisher or heat gun (optional) — restores clarity to sanded edges
  • Drill press or hand drill — for ventilation holes, bulkhead fittings, etc.
  • Step drill bit or spade bit — cleaner holes in acrylic than twist drills
  • Safety glasses — acrylic chips can fly during cutting

Process

Cutting Acrylic

Score-and-Snap (No Power Tools)

  1. Leave the masking on. Mark your cut line on the masking with a marker and straight edge.
  2. Clamp a straight edge along the cut line. Using a scoring tool or utility knife, score the line firmly — 5 to 10 passes, pressing hard. You want a groove at least 1/8" deep for clean snapping on ¼" stock.
  3. Position the scored line over the edge of a table or workbench. Hold the piece firmly on the table with one hand. Apply a sharp downward snap with the other. The piece breaks cleanly along the score line.
  4. Sand the edge smooth with 220-grit, then 320-grit. A clean, flat edge is critical for a strong solvent weld — gaps in the joint weaken the bond.

Saw Cutting

  1. Keep masking on. Apply a strip of masking tape along the cut line on both faces for extra chip protection.
  2. Use a fine-tooth blade (60+ teeth). Cut slowly — rushing generates heat that melts the acrylic and gums the blade.
  3. Support the piece on both sides of the cut so it doesn't flex and crack as you finish.
  4. Sand edges smooth before joining. Run them across a sheet of sandpaper on a flat surface (wet sanding works well) to ensure they're perfectly flat — a rocking or uneven edge makes a weak joint.

Drilling

Twist drill bits tend to grab and crack acrylic. Better options:

  • Step drill bits — designed for sheet materials, cleaner holes
  • Spade bits at low speed
  • Standard twist bits with the tip angle reduced to ~60° (re-grind or buy brad-point bits)

Always back the piece with a scrap of wood to prevent blowout on the exit side. Go slow. Let the bit do the work — don't force it.

Heat Is the Enemy Acrylic melts at relatively low temperatures. Too-fast cutting or drilling generates heat that melts and re-bonds the material behind the blade, creating a ragged edge that's hard to sand smooth and won't solvent-weld cleanly. Slow, steady cuts. Sharp blades only.

Process

Solvent Welding — The Right Way to Join Acrylic

Solvent cement doesn't glue acrylic — it temporarily liquefies the surfaces of both pieces, which then fuse together as the solvent evaporates. The result is one piece of material, not two pieces held together. Done correctly, the joint is as strong as (or stronger than) the base material.

Preparation Is Everything

The joint is only as good as the fit. Both surfaces must be flat, clean, and flush against each other before you apply any solvent. A gap in the joint means a weak spot. Sand edges on a flat surface until they lay perfectly flat together — hold them up to a light source; you shouldn't see light through the seam.

  1. Dry-fit everything first. Assemble your entire enclosure without solvent, held with masking tape or clamps, and check for square with a combination square. Fix any fit issues now — you can't fix them once the solvent goes in.
  2. Assemble in position. Get the pieces clamped or taped exactly where you want them. Work on a flat surface so gravity helps keep the joint closed.
  3. Apply solvent with the needle applicator. Hold the applicator bottle nearly horizontal along the joint seam. Touch the tip to the joint — capillary action pulls the solvent into the seam automatically. Move steadily along the full length of the joint. You should see the solvent wick in immediately. Use less than you think you need — excess solvent causes crazing (a frost/crack pattern) on the surface.
  4. Don't move it. Let the joint sit undisturbed for at least 5 minutes. Weld-On #3 and #4 set fast but take 24 hours to reach full strength. Handle gently for the first hour.
  5. Repeat for remaining joints. Do one joint at a time. Let each cure for a few minutes before repositioning for the next one.
Ventilation Required Weld-On solvent cement is flammable and the fumes are strong. Work outdoors or in a well-ventilated space. Don't use near open flame. The fumes dissipate quickly once applied but the bottle should be capped immediately after use.

The Typical Assembly Order (Box/Enclosure)

  1. Bottom panel flat on the work surface.
  2. Attach back wall to the bottom — apply solvent to the bottom edge of the back panel, hold in place, let cure.
  3. Attach the two side walls — both sides of the back and bottom joints get solvent.
  4. Attach the front wall.
  5. The top/lid is typically left removable or hinged rather than solvent welded, for access.
Joint Design: Butt vs. Rabbet The simplest joint is a butt joint — one panel sits on top of or against another. Stronger option is a rabbet joint, where you router a channel in one piece that the other fits into, creating more glue surface area and a mechanical interlock. For most homestead enclosures under 24" a butt joint with good surface prep is plenty strong.

Terrarium / Aquarium Build

Planning Your Cut List

For a box enclosure, you need five or six panels: bottom, back, two sides, front, and optionally a fixed top. The key math: account for panel thickness when sizing pieces so your overall outer dimensions come out right.

Example for a 24" L × 12" W × 12" H enclosure using ¼" acrylic:

PanelDimensionsNotes
Bottom24" × 12"Full footprint
Back24" × 12"Sits on top of bottom, between the sides
Two sides12" × 11.75" eachHeight minus bottom thickness (12" − ¼" = 11.75")
Front24" × 11.75"Same height as sides; can be hinged or removable
Top/lid24" × 12" (or with mesh)Usually left removable

Draw this out before ordering material. Have the supplier cut the sheet to size if possible — their panel saw is more accurate than most DIY cuts and saves time.

Ventilation

Most terrariums need airflow. Options:

  • Drill a grid of holes in the top or side panels (tedious but simple)
  • Cut a rectangular opening in the top and glue aluminum or stainless steel mesh over it from underneath — cleaner look and better airflow. Use Weld-On or E6000 to bond mesh to acrylic.
  • For aquariums or high-humidity builds, ventilation is less critical — you want to retain moisture.

Waterproofing for Aquariums

Solvent-welded joints are naturally waterproof when done correctly. For aquarium use or anything holding water, add a thin bead of aquarium-safe silicone along the inside of every joint as a secondary seal — not because the weld leaks, but as insurance. Let it cure 24–48 hours before adding water. Fill-test with plain water for 24 hours before use.

Front Access Options

The front panel is typically where you add access. Three common approaches:

MethodBest ForNotes
Sliding front panels Reptile/insect enclosures Cut two overlapping panels that slide in acrylic tracks solvent-welded to top and bottom. Simple, secure, no hardware.
Hinged door Larger enclosures, frequent access Use acrylic piano hinge solvent-welded to frame, or small stainless hinges with screws. Add a magnetic clasp or push-latch.
Removable top Aquariums, terrariums with mesh top Top just rests on the enclosure with no fastening — weight holds it in place. Simple but lifted animals can escape.

Troubleshooting

Common Problems & Fixes

ProblemCauseFix / Prevention
Crazing (frost/crack pattern near joints) Too much solvent applied, or solvent ran onto the face of the panel Use less solvent — a little goes a long way. Apply it precisely at the seam. Keep the needle tip in contact with the joint edge, not the surface. Can't be undone once it happens.
Weak or leaking joint Surfaces weren't flat/flush before cementing; gap in the joint Sand edges flatter and re-weld. For a leaking aquarium joint, dry it thoroughly, then apply silicone from the inside as a secondary seal.
Chipped or cracked edges when cutting Coarse blade, too much speed, or not supporting the piece Use finer blade; go slower; support both sides of cut. Chips on edges that will be hidden by joints don't matter — only visible edges need to be clean.
Acrylic cracked when drilling Drill bit grabbed; too fast; no backing material Always drill into a wood backer. Use a step bit or low-speed. Apply light pressure — let the bit cut rather than forcing it.
Surfaces scratched Masking removed too early; abrasive surfaces during build Keep masking on until final assembly. Minor scratches: use Novus Plastic Polish or automotive plastic restorer. Deep scratches can't be fully removed.
Not square Didn't check square during dry-fit or cementing Always check with a combination square before the solvent sets. Once cured, you can't adjust — the joint has to be cut apart and re-done.
Scratches Are the Main Maintenance Issue Acrylic scratches much more easily than glass. Use soft cloths only — never paper towels, which are abrasive enough to leave micro-scratches. A dedicated plastic cleaner like Novus #1 (polish) and #2 (fine scratch remover) is worth keeping on hand. Don't use Windex or ammonia-based cleaners — ammonia crazes acrylic over time.
