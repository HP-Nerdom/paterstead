Porch Garden

Potting Bench

Two approaches: build one from scratch with cedar and a garden hose sink, or remake a thrifted piece into something functional and beautiful.

Overview

Two Paths to the Same Goal

A potting bench gives you a dedicated, standing-height workspace for transplanting, starting seeds, cleaning tools, and staging plants — no more kneeling on the ground or carrying soil-covered pots inside. These two sources cover opposite ends of the effort spectrum.

ApproachEffortCostBest When
Build from scratch (cedar) Weekend project, basic woodworking ~$100–200 in lumber + hardware You want a custom size, a built-in sink, or specific storage layout
Furniture makeover A few hours, no power tools required $5–30 thrift find + paint + hardware You find the right piece at a yard sale and want a fast, charming result
What to Look for at Thrift Stores Changing tables are ideal repurpose candidates — already built for standing-height work, usually have a lower shelf, and show up at yard sales constantly for $10–30. Other good finds: hutches, bookshelves, kitchen islands, old workbenches, and console tables.

Approach 1 · Build From Scratch

Cedar Potting Bench with Sink

Source: The Handyman's Daughter — DIY Potting Bench with Optional Sink by Vineta Jackson

This build uses cedar (naturally rot-resistant and a great outdoor wood choice) assembled with a pocket hole jig. The sink connects to a standard garden hose — no plumbing required. The soil drop holes in the countertop are a smart detail: ⅝" holes drilled through the work surface so potting mix spills straight through into a container on the lower shelf instead of all over the floor.

Lumber

  • 2×4 cedar or pressure-treated boards — legs and frame
  • 1×6 cedar tongue-and-groove or flat boards — countertop (five boards planked together)
  • 1×6 cedar fence pickets — back slats and shelf

Hardware & Fasteners

  • Pocket hole jig (Kreg Jig R3 or K5)
  • 2½" outdoor pocket hole screws
  • Brad nailer + 1¼" brad nails
  • Titebond III or other waterproof wood glue

Sink Setup (optional)

  • Round stainless steel sink — architectural salvage yards often have these for a few dollars; also available new
  • Cold water faucet (single-handle; no hot water line needed)
  • Paddle/spade bit sized slightly larger than the faucet pipe
  • Garden hose to faucet adapter — this is how water gets to the bench; no indoor plumbing involved
  • Silicone adhesive — seals around sink and faucet
  • Bucket or large pot on the lower shelf to catch drain water (the drain runs into it, not into the ground)

Build Steps

  1. Build the countertop. Plank five 1×6 cedar boards together face-to-face using pocket holes and 1¼" pocket hole screws, cut to your desired length. Tongue-and-groove boards interlock for a tighter surface; flat boards work fine too. Target width: around 20–24" for a comfortable work surface depth.
  2. Cut the sink hole (optional). Trace the underside rim of your sink onto the countertop. Drill a starter hole just inside the line, then cut the opening with a jigsaw. Test-fit and trim tight spots until the sink drops in snugly with its rim resting on the surface.
  3. Support the sink. Cut short diagonal scrap pieces of 1×2 cedar and attach them underneath the countertop around the sink hole — these give the sink rim something to rest on. Add two longer crosspieces underneath the full countertop to keep the planked boards flat and tight.
  4. Add soil drop holes (optional but smart). On the side of the countertop away from the sink, drill a cluster of ⅝" holes. Soil that spills while potting falls through into a lidded container on the shelf below — keeps the surface cleaner and nothing is wasted.
  5. Drill the faucet hole. Use a paddle bit sized to your faucet pipe and drill through the countertop near the sink. Drop the faucet in and secure with its mounting hardware underneath.
  6. Build the frames. The countertop frame and the lower shelf frame are identical rectangles — cut all frame pieces at once and drill pocket holes in both ends of the short pieces. Assemble each frame with 2½" outdoor pocket hole screws. The frames should match the outside dimensions of the countertop and shelf panels.
  7. Attach the shelf panel. Plank the shelf boards the same way as the countertop. Attach the shelf panel to its frame with brad nails and glue.
  8. Attach legs. Cut four 2×4 legs to your desired height (typically 34–36" for a comfortable standing work height). Attach the countertop frame and shelf frame to the legs using pocket hole screws — position the shelf at whatever height works for your storage containers.
  9. Add back slats. Cut 1×6 fence pickets to height and attach them as vertical back slats using brad nails and glue. Leave small gaps between slats for drainage and airflow. The back also serves as a hanging surface for tools.
  10. Install sink and faucet. Drop the sink into its hole and run a bead of silicone around the rim seal. Mount the faucet through its hole. Underneath: connect the garden hose adapter to the faucet supply line. When you want water, connect your garden hose to the adapter on the back of the bench. Drain water falls into the bucket below.
Cedar vs. Pressure-Treated Cedar is naturally rot-resistant, lighter, and looks nicer — the preferred choice for a visible porch bench. Pressure-treated lumber is cheaper and more durable but heavier, harder to work with, and contains preservative chemicals you may not want around edible plants. If the bench will be covered and out of direct weather, cedar is fine unfinished; if exposed, seal it with an exterior wood oil or finish after building.

Approach 2 · Furniture Makeover

Thrifted Piece → Potting Bench

Source: Atta Girl Says — DIY Potting Bench Makeover by Amy (Atta Girl Amy)

This approach starts with a yard sale changing table (about $5–15 at a good sale) and transforms it with paint, hardware swaps, and a few functional additions. No power tools needed for the core project. The result is something with a lot more personality than a plain cedar build and takes a few hours instead of a weekend.

The Paint Process

  1. Sand the existing finish. Don't strip entirely unless the paint is peeling badly — a thorough rough sanding is enough to give the new paint something to grip. Remove any loose or flaking material.
  2. Base coat in chalk paint. An olive green or earthy tone works well as a base that will show through distressing later. Chalk paint is forgiving, dries fast, and adheres to most surfaces without primer. One coat is enough — this is intentionally going to be partially covered.
  3. Top coat in your main color. Apply two coats of flat latex exterior paint in your chosen color. Flat finish hides imperfections better than satin and is easier to distress.
  4. Distress lightly. Once fully dry, lightly sand corners, edges, and raised areas to let the base coat color show through. This creates a worn, vintage look that suits a garden piece well. Don't overdo it.
  5. Seal with exterior polyurethane. Several coats of poly protect the paint from soil, water, and weather. This is what makes a painted piece survive outdoors — don't skip it.

Hardware Swaps — The Detail That Makes It

Replacing standard cabinet knobs with outdoor faucet handles (the cross-shaped kind) is the signature touch in this makeover. They're functional as pulls, look fantastic on a garden piece, and cost almost nothing at yard sales. Check Habitat for Humanity ReStores and estate sales — old faucet handles turn up constantly for $0.25–$1 each. Hobby Lobby also stocks decorative versions.

Other Hardware Ideas Small galvanized hooks for hanging hand tools. Vintage drawer pulls from salvage shops. Cabinet hardware shaped like leaves, acorns, or insects. The hardware is the fastest way to push the piece from "painted furniture" to "garden piece."

The Galvanized Potting Surface

A galvanized corn dryer (a flat galvanized metal tray with drainage holes) makes an excellent potting surface — it's durable, cleans up easily, and looks right at home on a garden bench. Attach it to the top of the piece with hinges at the back so it tilts up for dumping soil off the front. A few details:

  • Standard corn dryers are typically around 24"×36" — measure your bench top first.
  • Two heavy-duty hinges on the back edge hold it down during use and let it tip forward to clear soil.
  • The tray's drainage holes let water through — put a tray underneath or angle the whole surface slightly toward the front to drain away from the piece.
  • Other options: galvanized utility trays, metal baking sheets, or a zinc-coated worktop.

Cabinet Door Decoupage (Optional)

Decoupaging the inside of a cabinet door with photocopies of old seed packet labels is a charming finishing touch that's easy and cheap. Use Mod Podge as both the adhesive and the sealant — apply it under and over the paper cutouts. Black-and-white photocopies of vintage seed packets, botanical illustrations, or garden catalog pages all work well and cost almost nothing to print.

Makeover Supply List

  • Thrifted changing table or cabinet — $5–30
  • Sandpaper — 80 grit to scuff, 150–220 to smooth
  • Chalk paint in a base color (olive green, mustard, rust)
  • Flat exterior latex paint in main color
  • Exterior polyurethane — at least 3 coats
  • Paintbrushes + foam roller
  • Replacement hardware: outdoor faucet handles as pulls, hooks, knobs
  • Galvanized tray for potting surface
  • 2 heavy-duty hinges (to attach tray)
  • Mod Podge + seed packet photocopies (for inside cabinet doors)

Planning

Features to Plan For

Whether building from scratch or reworking a thrifted piece, think through these before you start:

FeatureHow to Add ItWorth It?
Sink + garden hose faucet Drop-in stainless sink + hose-to-faucet adapter; drain into a bucket below Yes if you do a lot of washing — harvest cleanup, hand washing, rinsing pots
Soil drop holes Drill ⅝" holes in countertop over a lidded container below Yes — keeps the work surface cleaner and saves loose soil
Lower shelf storage Add during build, or use existing shelf on thrifted piece Essential — holds soil bags, pots, watering cans, the drain bucket
Back panel with hooks Back slats + S-hooks or individual mounted hooks Yes — keeps hand tools, gloves, and twine off the work surface
Side bins or shelves Small cedar bins or galvanized buckets hung off the sides Good for small pots, fertilizer, seed packets
Wheels / casters Add locking casters to the legs at build time Useful if you want to move the bench seasonally or follow the sun
Power strip Mount a weatherproof outlet strip to the back panel; run to porch outlet Handy for grow lights, heat mats, or charging tools
Porch-Specific Considerations for Zone 6b A covered porch protects the bench from direct rain but not from temperature swings. Cedar or sealed pressure-treated wood handles Zone 6b winters fine if protected from standing water and allowed to dry out between wet periods. Bring any galvanized trays in over winter if possible — they'll rust over many seasons if left wet. The garden hose connection means zero winterizing needed: just disconnect the hose and coil it in the shed.
