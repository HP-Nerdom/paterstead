Fermentation

Homemade Hard Cider

7–8 bottles from one gallon of apple juice. About $10–20 in equipment, most of it reusable for years.

Overview

What You're Getting Into

This guide is for making one gallon of hard cider — roughly 7–8 sixteen-ounce bottles. It's a great starting size because mistakes are small and cheap. The recipe is expandable once you've got the process down.

Is It Legal? Yes. In the U.S., every adult in your home may brew up to 100 gallons of wine, beer, or cider per year for personal use — no license needed. Just don't sell it.
FactorDetail
Yield (1 gallon batch)7–8 sixteen-oz bottles
Total equipment cost~$10–20 upfront; reusable for years

Setup

Equipment List

Essential (for 1 gallon)

  • Two 1-gallon glass jugs — one for primary fermentation, one for secondary. If your juice came in a glass jug, that counts as one.
  • Rubber stopper with hole — size #6 for a 1-gallon jug
  • Airlock (also called a "bubbler") — fits into the stopper hole
  • Brewing yeast — NOT bread yeast (see yeast section)
  • Star San sanitizer — 1 oz makes 5 gallons of sanitizer solution; lasts 3–4 weeks mixed
  • 4–6 feet of 5/16" food-grade plastic tubing — for racking (siphoning) between jugs
  • 1 Campden tablet — only needed if using fresh, unpasteurized cider

Highly Recommended

  • Auto-siphon — ~$9; makes racking dramatically easier and less messy
  • Bottle filler hose extension — ~$7; prevents mess when filling bottles
  • Hydrometer — ~$6; measures sugar content so you know exactly what your cider is doing and when to bottle. Worth getting if you make more than one batch.

For Bottling (if carbonating)

  • Flip-top (Grolsch-style) bottles — reusable, no capper needed
  • OR standard beer bottles + bottle caps + bottle capper
  • For sweetness without re-fermentation: Stevia or Xylitol (yeast can't eat these, so they sweeten without adding carbonation)
  • For back-carbonation: brown sugar, dextrose (corn sugar), or frozen apple juice concentrate
Use Glass, Not Plastic, for Fermentation If your juice came in a plastic jug, transfer it to a glass jug before adding yeast. Plastic is fine for storing finished cider but fermentation needs glass — plastic scratches easily and harbors bacteria in those scratches.

Critical First Step

Clean & Sterile

The biggest mistake first-time brewers make is skipping or rushing sanitation. Contamination turns your cider to vinegar. Everything that touches your juice — jugs, tubing, stoppers, spoons, funnels, your hands — must be cleaned and then sanitized.

Cleaning (removing what you can see)

  1. Soak all equipment in an oxidizing cleaner (like PBW by Five Star) or hot soapy water for at least 15 minutes. Soak tubing for a full hour to let the solution work inside.
  2. Scrub with a bottle brush — bottles and jugs have hidden crevices.
  3. Rinse thoroughly with clean water. No soap taste in your cider.

Sanitizing (removing what you can't see)

Star San is the recommended sanitizer. It's acid-based, odorless, and breaks down into a simple sugar that actually feeds yeast — so trace amounts won't hurt your brew. Mix 1 oz per 5 gallons of water. One mixed batch stays effective for 3–4 weeks.

  1. Soak everything in Star San solution for at least 2 minutes. Glass jugs: 15 minutes minimum.
  2. Do NOT rinse with tap water afterward — this re-introduces bacteria. Tilt and drain as much solution as possible, then do a quick swish-rinse with a small amount of bottled or freshly boiled water if desired.
  3. Stage sanitized bottles upside-down in a freshly run dishwasher (no soap cycle needed — just the heat).
Don't Use Bleach or Iodine Bleach destroys plastic and rubber equipment and leaves off-flavors if not rinsed perfectly. Iodine stains everything yellow and has the same taste risk. Star San avoids both problems.

Ingredients

Choosing Your Apple Juice

The juice is the foundation of the flavor. You have three options:

1. Pasteurized Orchard Cider (Best Choice)

Fresh-pressed cider from a local orchard that has been pasteurized — either with heat or UV light. Full apple flavor, no preservatives, no food safety risk. Ask the orchard whether their cider has chemical preservatives — many now do. If they don't, this is your best option. Tell them you're making hard cider; they'll often have tips.

2. Grocery Store Apple Juice / Cider

Works well — but you must check the label carefully. The juice needs to be cloudy (not clear), with sediment. You want something that looks like real cider, not Mott's apple juice.

Preservatives Will Kill Your Fermentation Potassium sorbate and sodium benzoate prevent yeast from growing — meaning your cider will never ferment. Check every label. The only safe additives are ascorbic acid (vitamin C) and malic acid (natural apple acid) — both are fine. Brands that have worked: Musselman's Apple Cider, Whole Foods 365 Organic Apple Juice, Santa Cruz Organics.

3. Raw Unpasteurized Cider (Use With Caution)

Best flavor, but carries a genuine food safety risk from E. coli and other pathogens from wildlife contamination at orchards. If you use raw cider, use a Campden tablet (step 2 below) to suppress wild bacteria and wild yeast before pitching your own yeast. Fermenting does NOT make contaminated juice safe — the alcohol content in cider is too low to kill pathogens.

Quick Label Guide Look for: cloudy brown juice, sediment at the bottom, no listed preservatives beyond ascorbic acid or malic acid. Avoid: clear crystal-clean "apple juice," anything listing potassium sorbate or sodium benzoate, anything labeled "to preserve freshness" followed by chemical names.

Ingredients

Yeast Selection

Use brewing yeast, not bread yeast. Bread yeast is a completely different strain optimized for making dough rise, not for producing clean-tasting alcohol. One 5g packet of brewing yeast is enough for 5 gallons — use about 1/5 of the packet per gallon.

YeastBest ForNotes
Nottingham Ale Yeast Top recommendation overall Fruity, draft-ale character; ferments well at 55–65°F; sinks and clarifies the cider; no yeasty aftertaste. Use in fall/winter when temps are cooler. 11g packet does up to 12 gallons.
Lalvin EC-1118 (Champagne) Warm rooms, fast fermentation Very vigorous — out-competes wild yeast. Eats all sugar fast, carbonates quickly. Good if your brew space is above 65°F. Produces a drier, crisper cider.
Safcider 40725 Preserving apple flavor Highly regarded on cider forums for holding onto natural apple character. Works across a wide temperature range (50–86°F). Good versatile choice.
White Labs WLP775 English Cider Traditional English-style Purpose-built cider yeast. Produces a classic dry English cider profile.
Wild Yeast (Optional Adventure) You can skip adding yeast entirely and let wild airborne yeast ferment the juice naturally — especially if using fresh cider mill juice that already has orchard yeast in it. Results are unpredictable: could be fantastic, could be terrible. Not recommended for first batches.

Process

Making One Gallon — Step by Step

  1. Get your juice. Purchase 1 gallon of preservative-free apple cider or juice. If it's in plastic, you'll need to transfer it to a sanitized glass jug before fermenting.
  2. Campden tablet (fresh/raw cider only). Crush one Campden tablet, dissolve in 1 Tbsp very hot water, and add to the juice. Cover loosely with cheesecloth or a paper towel secured with a rubber band. Wait 48 hours, shaking a couple times a day to release the sulfur gas. Skip this step entirely if using pasteurized grocery store juice.
  3. Make sure your juice is in a glass jug. Transfer from plastic if needed using a sanitized funnel.
  4. Optional — add pectic enzyme. A pinch clears up haze by breaking down pectin. Doesn't affect taste, only appearance. Not required.
  5. Optional flavor and alcohol additions. Add any combination of the following before pitching yeast:
    • › ¼ of a 12oz can of frozen apple juice concentrate — boosts alcohol to ~5–6% and adds apple body (especially helpful with grocery-store juice)
    • › ¼ cup brown sugar (instead of concentrate) — similar alcohol boost, slightly different character
    • › ¼ tsp acid blend (malic + tannic acid) — adds complexity and "bite" similar to traditional cider apples
    • › ¼ tsp yeast nutrient — B-vitamins that help yeast thrive in apple juice (which is naturally low in yeast nutrients)

    Do NOT add spices at this stage — yeast will eat and transform them into off-flavors. Add spices or flavorings only after fermentation is complete.

  6. Pitch the yeast. Sprinkle about 1/5 of a 5g yeast packet on top of the juice. Don't stir — just let it rest. Make sure no grains are stuck on the jug neck; gently swirl if so.
  7. Fit the airlock. Press the sanitized rubber stopper (#6 for a 1-gallon jug) into the jug neck. Insert the sanitized airlock into the stopper hole. Fill the airlock halfway with water or Star San solution. This lets CO2 escape during fermentation while keeping air and contaminants out.
  8. Ferment. Store in a location with stable temperature — ideally 55–65°F for Nottingham yeast (cooler = better flavor, slower process), or 65–75°F for EC-1118. Within 24–48 hours you'll see bubbles blooping through the airlock. Primary fermentation takes approximately 2 weeks. Leave it alone.
Alcohol Content Guide No added sugar → ~3.5–4.5% ABV. Plus ¼ can frozen concentrate → ~5–6%. Plus concentrate AND ½ cup brown sugar → ~7–8%. Beyond that you're making apple wine.

Secondary Fermentation

Racking (Moving to a Clean Jug)

After primary fermentation slows (about 2 weeks), you'll see sediment at the bottom called "lees" — dead yeast and fruit solids. Left in contact with the cider, lees add bitter off-flavors. Racking moves the clear cider off the lees into a clean jug.

Equipment for Racking

  • Primary jug (with the cider)
  • Second clean, sanitized 1-gallon glass jug (the secondary)
  • 4–6 feet of sanitized food-grade tubing
  • Auto-siphon — strongly recommended; makes this step easy and clean
  • Optional: tubing clamp to stop flow mid-rack without losing suction
  1. Clean and sanitize the secondary jug and all tubing.
  2. Set the primary jug (with cider) on a counter. Set the empty secondary jug on the floor directly below it — gravity is your pump.
  3. Insert the auto-siphon into the primary jug, keeping the tip above the lees layer at the bottom. Give it one pump to start the siphon.
  4. Let the cider flow into the secondary jug. Stop when you're close to the lees — leave the bottom inch or so of murky sediment behind in the primary jug.
  5. Fit a clean stopper and airlock on the secondary jug. Let it sit for another 2 weeks. The cider will continue fermenting slowly and clearing.
Practice First If you've never siphoned before, practice with plain water before touching your cider. The auto-siphon makes it much easier than the old mouth-priming method — worth the $9.

Bottling

Carbonation & Bottling

You have three choices: still (flat), back-carbonated (fizzy, controlled), or naturally carbonated by catching the fermentation just before it finishes.

Still Cider (Easiest)

Let fermentation complete fully — all bubbling stops and the cider tastes dry. Rack to secondary, let clear, and bottle. No extra sugar needed. Add Stevia or Xylitol to sweeten if desired (yeast can't ferment these, so they sweeten without re-activating carbonation). Bottle in any container.

Back Carbonation (Most Controlled — Recommended)

Wait until fermentation is fully complete and the cider is dry. Then add a small, measured amount of fermentable sugar before bottling. The remaining yeast in the bottles eat this sugar and produce CO2, carbonating the cider in the sealed bottle over about 2 weeks.

Back Carbonation Sugar Amounts (per gallon)

  • ⅛–¼ cup brown sugar dissolved in ½ cup boiled water, OR
  • ¼ of a 12oz can of frozen apple juice concentrate, OR
  • ⅛ cup dextrose (corn sugar) — gives finer, more consistent bubbles
  • Alternatively: ½ tsp dextrose added directly into each pint bottle at filling
Don't Over-Carbonate More sugar = more carbonation = potential bottle explosions. Use measured amounts. After 2 weeks, open one bottle to test. If it's under-carbonated, wait another week. If it's already very fizzy, refrigerate all bottles immediately to stop the yeast. Never use screw-top (twist-off) bottles — the glass neck is too thin and can crack under pressure. Use flip-top or standard beer bottles only.

Adding Sweetness

Adding regular sugar to finished cider to sweeten it will just re-start fermentation and create more carbonation (or bottle bombs). To make a finished cider taste sweeter without carbonation effects, use zero-calorie sweeteners that yeast can't eat: Stevia, Xylitol, or standard artificial sweeteners. Add to taste just before bottling.

Xylitol Is Toxic to Dogs Keep xylitol-sweetened cider out of reach of pets.

Bottling Process

  1. Clean and sanitize all bottles.
  2. If back-carbonating, dissolve your sugar in a small amount of boiled water and stir gently into the secondary jug before bottling (or add direct to each bottle).
  3. Rack the cider from the secondary jug into bottles using the siphon and tubing. A bottle filler hose extension with a spring-tip valve makes this much cleaner — the flow stops when you lift the tube off the bottle bottom.
  4. Cap bottles immediately. For flip-tops, just close the wire bail. For standard beer bottles, use a bench capper.
  5. Store at room temperature for ~2 weeks to carbonate. Then refrigerate and enjoy.
