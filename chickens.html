DIY PVC Chicken Feeder

Source: Instructables — PVC Chicken Feeder by mikecraghead (568k views, Featured)

Easy to fill, hard to spill, weather resistant, pest resistant, and takes about 3 minutes to assemble. One 10-foot section of 3" pipe makes at least three feeders. The key detail that makes this design work: a 3" extension piece below the Y-connector. Without it, chickens spill constantly. With it, spillage drops to nearly zero.

Materials

  • 3" diameter PVC pipe — 20" long (the main storage tube; longer = more capacity)
  • 3" diameter PVC pipe — 6" long (bottom feeding section below the Y)
  • 3" diameter PVC pipe — 3" long (the anti-spill extension; don't skip this)
  • 45° PVC Y-connector, 3"
  • Two 3" PVC end caps — one removable top for filling, one bottom for weatherproofing
  • PVC cement (or leave unglued for easier cleaning — just be careful not to drop a full feeder)
  • Hacksaw
  1. Assemble the Y section. Connect the 45° Y-connector to the bottom of the 20" main tube. Attach the 6" feeding section to the downward-angled port of the Y. Attach the 3" anti-spill extension below that — this short piece pointing slightly forward is what stops the birds from raking feed out onto the ground.
  2. Glue and cure. Apply PVC cement to the three joints that touch the Y-connector and the bottom cap. Let cure 24 hours. (The top cap stays removable for filling.)
  3. Mount. Bungee the feeder to the coop wall or a post. The feeder can sit on a brick to raise the feed port to chicken head height — they eat more comfortably and spill less when the port is at the right level.
  4. Fill and cap. Remove the top cap and fill using a funnel. Replace the cap when done. At night, place the cap over the bottom port opening as well to keep the feed weather-proof and critter-proof.
The 3" Extension Is the Whole Secret The builder tried a 180° elbow, a plain Y with no extension, and several other configurations. The 3" piece below the Y is the only thing that solved spillage. Don't leave it out.

Project · Add-On

PVC Feeder Level Meter

Source: Instructables — PVC Chicken Feeder with Meter by flodato (94k views, Featured)

An add-on to the Y-connector feeder above that lets you check the feed level from outside the coop without opening anything. A float weight sits on top of the feed inside the pipe, connected by weed whacker wire through a hole in the coop wall to a small external indicator. As feed level drops, the float drops, and the outside indicator rises — running on "E" is instantly visible.

The builder reports three weeks of use without issues, and his kids now check and report the feed level themselves.

Additional Materials Needed

  • Weed whacker wire — doesn't fray like twine, doesn't snag like string
  • A lightweight float weight — any small lightweight object that fits inside the pipe and can rest on top of the feed (a water bottle weighted slightly works well; a small piece of a handle, a film canister, etc.)
  • A lightweight external indicator — something that will hang visibly outside the coop and rise as the float drops; needs to be light enough that the feed weight pulls it up easily
  • Drill + small drill bit
  1. Drill a small hole in the top cap. Size it so weed whacker wire slides through with minimal friction but doesn't have much slop.
  2. Thread the wire. Run the wire through the hole in the cap, down into the feeder tube. Tie or secure the float weight to the bottom end of the wire so it rests on top of the feed inside the pipe.
  3. Drill through the coop wall. Run the other end of the wire through a small hole in the coop wall to the outside.
  4. Attach the external indicator. Tie the lightweight indicator object to the outside end of the wire. When the feeder is full, the float is high inside the tube and the indicator hangs low outside. As the feed drops, so does the float — and the indicator rises toward the wall as a visual "getting low" signal.
  5. Mark the levels. Fill the feeder completely and mark the external indicator position as "Full." Empty it halfway and mark "Half." Empty completely and mark "E." Those three marks are your gauge.
Wire Choice Matters Twine frays and gets soggy. Butcher string can catch on rough wood edges and jam. Weed whacker monofilament line is the right call — stiff enough to move freely, smooth enough not to snag, and weatherproof.

Fermented Chicken Feed

Fermenting soaks feed in water to allow beneficial bacteria to grow. This pre-digests the grains, improves nutrient bioavailability, and makes feed easier to digest — chickens typically eat less of it while getting more nutrition.

What You Need

  • Non-metal container (bucket, large jar, or food-grade tub)
  • Clean, non-chlorinated water (or let tap water sit 24 hours)
  • Stirring utensil
  • Loose-fitting lid or breathable cover
  • Dry chicken feed — pellets, crumble, scratch grains, or whole grains
Avoid Feeds with added medication (like medicated chick starter) — fermentation may reduce the medication's effectiveness.
  1. Mix feed and water. Add dry feed to your container. Cover with water until the feed is fully submerged, about 1–2 inches above the feed line. Stir thoroughly.
  2. Ferment 2–3 days. Cover loosely (cloth, paper towel, or unsealed lid). Leave at room temperature out of direct sunlight. Stir once or twice daily. It should begin to bubble slightly and smell mildly sour or yeasty — like sourdough. Warmer temps = faster ferment.
  3. Feed. Scoop out the top portion. Drain excess water if desired. Serve in a clean dish. Only give what they'll eat in 30–45 minutes to avoid spoilage.
  4. Maintain the ferment. Top off with more feed and water after each scoop. Clean out and restart the batch every 1–2 weeks.
Cold Weather Tip Ferment indoors or in a sheltered area. You can use warm (not hot) water for mixing in winter to keep the culture active.
If It Goes Bad If it smells rotten or moldy (not just sour), discard and start fresh. Introduce fermented feed gradually over a few days to avoid digestive upset in the flock.

Processing

Plucking & Processing Chickens

Equipment

  • Killing cone or restraining method
  • Sharp knife or poultry shears
  • Large pot or scalder — water heated to 145–155°F (63–68°C)
  • Thermometer
  • Plucking machine (optional)
  • Clean processing table
  • Bucket or container for feathers and waste
  • Garden hose or water source
  • Ice chest or cold water bath
  • Gloves (optional but recommended)

Setup

You need separate stations for: killing, scalding, plucking, evisceration (gutting), and chilling. Set up all stations before you start. Heat water in your scalder to 145–155°F and prepare a large cold water + ice bath for chilling.

  1. Humane slaughter. Place the chicken in the killing cone head-first — this calms the bird. Pull the head through the bottom. Make a clean cut on both sides of the neck, severing the carotid arteries and jugular veins. Let the bird bleed out completely, about 1–2 minutes.
  2. Scalding. Dip the bird in hot water (145–155°F) for 30–60 seconds. Agitate gently to ensure water penetrates the feathers. Test by pulling wing feathers — they should come off easily.
  3. Plucking. By hand: start with wing and tail feathers, work from tail to head. With a machine: drop the bird in and run 10–30 seconds. Rinse with cold water afterward.
  4. Evisceration (gutting). Remove head and feet. Make a small cut at the vent (anus) and carefully enlarge the opening. Reach inside and pull out internal organs in one motion. Set aside liver, heart, and gizzard if keeping. Avoid rupturing the gallbladder (green sac on liver). Remove lungs. Flush the cavity with clean cold water.
  5. Chilling. Place the cleaned bird in the ice water bath immediately. Let it soak until internal temperature drops below 40°F (4°C) — about 30 minutes to 1 hour.
  6. Rest and package. Rest the bird in the fridge for 12–24 hours before freezing or cooking. This improves texture significantly. Package and freeze if storing long term.
Tips Sanitize all tools and surfaces before and after. Use clean, potable water throughout. Compost feathers and guts, or dispose of responsibly.

Project · Variant

Wall-Mount PVC Feeder

A simpler alternative to the hanging Y-connector feeder. A 4" diameter PVC pipe (50–100cm long) is cut lengthwise to create an open trough that mounts directly to the coop wall. No Y-connector, no tray — just pipe, a saw, and two screws.

Materials

  • 4" (100mm) PVC drainage or ventilation pipe, 50–100cm long
  • Angle grinder or jigsaw (grinder gives cleaner, splinter-free edges)
  • Sandpaper to smooth edges
  • Wood screws or pipe clips to mount to coop wall
  1. Mark the cut line. Use a permanent marker and a flat table to draw a straight line down the length of the pipe. Cut along this line to split the pipe in half lengthwise — you now have a trough shape.
  2. Smooth the edges. Sand all cut edges smooth so birds don't cut themselves.
  3. Mount to wall. Attach to the coop wall with wood screws or pipe clips at a comfortable height for your birds. Fill with feed.
Why This Works No moving parts, nothing to clog, and easy to wipe clean. Best for pellets or crumble — scratch grains will spill more easily from an open trough than a vertical pipe design.

Fodder System Notes for Chickens

The full fodder system guide is on the Fodder page. These are chicken-specific notes from Prairie Paterstead's setup.

Tray Setup (Prairie Paterstead Method)

PVC pipe rack built to fit standard 10"×20" heavy-duty seedling trays. Each tray has 12–16 small holes drilled in the bottom for drainage. Use heavy-duty trays — standard flimsy trays crack at the corners after a few uses under the weight of the fodder mat.

Soaking Trick

Drill holes in the bottom of a 5-gallon bucket and nest it inside a second bucket. Soak the grain in the inner bucket; at the end of the soak period, lift the inner bucket and the water drains automatically into the outer one. After the grain settles and sinks, agitate it — the chaff floats to the top. Skim it off since it won't sprout and you don't want it in your trays.

Watering

Water gently 4 times per day — seeds stay moist but no standing water in trays. Standing water causes mold, slimy mats, and foul odor. Avoid recirculating the water back through the trays for the same reason. Use a fresh 5-gallon bucket of clean water as the pump source and drain used water weekly (into the garden — it's nutrient-rich).

Grain Options

Barley, wheat, oats, and even sunflower seeds all work. Wheat is common because it's widely available from farms and mills. Check True Leaf Market online if local sourcing isn't an option. Aim for clean, quality grain — chaff percentage affects your yield.

Yield Rule About 5 lb of dry wheat yields ~25 lb of finished fodder. Feed at 1–2% of body weight per day. Source: Prairie Paterstead / Celtic Prairie Farm.

Feed · Additional Tips

Fermented Feed — BackYard Chickens Tips

The full fermented feed guide is above. These are additional practical notes from the BackYard Chickens community.

How to Tell It's Ready

A finished ferment smells mildly sour or faintly fruity. The clearest sign is bubbles — when you stir normal feed in water, a few bubbles come up. When the ferment is ready, it bubbles excessively at the slightest stir. You'll know it when you see it. Ready time is around day 2–3 (24–48 hours after starting).

Feeding Method

Scoop out a portion, drain most of the liquid back into the bucket (chickens usually don't drink the juice separately), and serve in a shallow container. Pie plates, silicone loaf pans, or a rain gutter trough all work well. Fermented feed won't flow through a standard tube or gravity feeder — it needs an open dish.

Transitioning the Flock

Chickens used to 24/7 dry feed access may refuse fermented feed at first. Cut off dry feed access completely. It may take a couple of days for them to learn to eat at designated meal times rather than grazing all day. Be patient — once they get used to it, most flocks prefer it over dry feed.

Benefits (from the BYC article)

  • › Easier to digest, especially pellets and crumbles
  • › Chickens eat less — food expands and is digested more thoroughly
  • › Less spillage — wet feed is harder to fling out of dishes
  • › Hens lay more eggs with thicker shells (per community reports)
  • › Ongoing — just top off with fresh feed and water after each feeding

DIY Chicken Plucker

A mechanical plucker removes feathers in under a minute versus 5–10 minutes by hand per bird. Two practical DIY approaches depending on budget and how many birds you process.

↗
Instructables: Make a Chicken/Duck/Bird Plucker (3 steps)

Option A — Drill-Attachment Barrel Plucker (Instructables Build)

Source: Instructables — Make a Chicken/Duck/Bird Plucker by tytower

A 4" drainage pipe cylinder with 24 rubber fingers, driven by a variable-speed electric drill. The whole unit mounts in a small plywood holder box clamped to a sawhorse. Cheap, compact, and handles chickens, ducks, and other birds.

Materials

  • 4" (100mm) PVC drainage pipe — 1 foot long
  • Two timber/plywood end caps, 4" diameter, ¾" thick
  • Wood glue and screws
  • 24 rubber plucking fingers — order from AliExpress or eBay (allow 3–4 weeks for delivery)
  • ½" steel rod, ~18" long — the drive shaft
  • Steel rectangle plate with a center hole (to fit the rod) and two side holes (to screw into timber) — welded to the drive rod end
  • Variable-speed electric drill with hold/lock button on the trigger
  • Plywood for the holder box — two end pieces, a bottom, and a couple of corner battens
  • Copper saddle or metal bracket — to secure the drill body in the holder box
  • Hacksaw, drill, jigsaw

Build the Barrel

  1. Cut two circular timber plugs sized to fit snugly inside the pipe ends. Glue and screw one end in permanently. The other end is screwed in temporarily for now.
  2. Drill a center hole in each end cap, sized to pass the ½" steel rod through.
  3. Divide the barrel into 6 equal sections (every 60°). Draw a line end-to-end along each mark. Drill 4 finger holes along each line — 24 holes total. Stagger alternate lines by ¾" so fingers are offset rather than all in a row (this makes plucking much more effective).
  4. Measure the inner groove diameter of your rubber fingers. Drill all 24 holes to exactly that dimension. Apply a little rubber grease to each finger's stem, then push each through its hole from inside the barrel until the groove snaps into place. Glue the second end cap in now and re-screw it.

Build the Drive Shaft

  1. Thread the ½" steel rod through the center of the barrel so one end protrudes about 2" (this is the drill end). The other end is as long as you need for the holder.
  2. Place the steel rectangle plate over the short drill end — center hole over the rod, side holes aligned with the timber end cap. Tack weld in place, then pull apart and permanently weld once you know the alignment is right.
  3. Grind a flat face (or hex flats) onto the drill end of the rod so the drill chuck bites properly and doesn't slip.

Build the Holder Box

  1. Cut two plywood end pieces and a bottom piece. Glue and screw corner battens to the end pieces, then attach to the bottom. Let it set for a day or two.
  2. On one end piece, cut a half-circle with a jigsaw shaped to cradle the neck of your specific drill. On the other end, drill a hole or use a saddle to secure the long end of the steel rod.
  3. Secure the drill with a copper saddle (or metal bracket) screwed over the top of the drill body to hold it in the box. The rod end similarly gets a saddle — no half-circle needed there, just a bracket over it.

How to Use

  1. Clamp the holder box to a sawhorse or solid surface.
  2. Chuck the drill onto the drive rod. Turn on the drill, set to a comfortable speed, engage the trigger lock so it runs hands-free.
  3. Scald the bird first: dip in hot water at a ratio of 2 parts boiling to 1 part cold. Count to about 10 seconds, then test a wing feather — it should pull out easily.
  4. Hold the bird against the spinning fingers. Adjust scald time and plucker speed to find what works for your birds. Heavier breeds may need a longer scald.

Option B — Barrel/Tub Plucker (Higher Volume)

A barrel or tub with rubber fingers mounted on a spinning plate inside. Drop the bird in, run for 30–60 seconds, done. Handles multiple birds per batch and saves significant labor on a larger processing day. Costs $100–300 DIY vs $750+ for a commercial unit.

Key Components

  • 55-gallon food-grade plastic barrel or large plastic tub
  • ~100 rubber plucking fingers — the main expense; quality matters
  • Marine plywood disk (finger plate) cut to fit inside barrel
  • ½ HP motor (a ¾–1 HP is recommended; ½ HP works for 1–2 birds at a time)
  • V-belt and pulleys, or direct drive setup
  • 2×4 wooden frame to support and elevate the barrel
  • Kill switch within arm's reach
Common Mistakes Cheap rubber fingers harden in water and stop working — budget for decent quality. Uneven finger spacing causes feather residue. Motor brackets need to be permanently bolted, not temporarily attached. Always install a kill switch you can reach while operating.
Scalding Is Still Required No plucker — DIY or commercial — removes feathers from an unscalded bird well. Proper scalding (145–155°F for 30–60 seconds) is what makes feathers release. The plucker just saves you from doing that work by hand.

Housing

Plastic Shed Chicken Coop

Converting a resin or plastic storage shed (Rubbermaid, Suncast, etc.) into a chicken coop. The appeal: never rots, never needs painting, cleans out completely with a garden hose, and large enough to walk into for easy maintenance.

↗
BackYard Chickens: Plastic Shed Chicken Coop — full build article

Why Plastic Over Wood

  • › No rot, no mold issues with proper ventilation
  • › No repainting ever
  • › Hoses out completely for deep cleaning
  • › Plastic is naturally predator-resistant — nothing chews through it easily
  • › Easy to disinfect between flocks

Key Modifications Needed

Ventilation (most critical)

  • Cut vents near the roofline — heat and ammonia must escape from the top
  • Use dryer-vent covers or off-the-shelf plastic wall vents inserted into cut holes with a short metal duct section; cap inside with a flat round vent
  • For hot days, a solar-powered fan vented high on the wall works well
  • Plastic sheds run 10–20°F warmer than outside — ventilation is more important than in a wood coop
  • Cover all vent openings with ½" hardware cloth, not chicken wire (predators can reach through or tear chicken wire)

Interior Setup

  • Roosts must be higher than nest boxes — birds sleep on the highest available perch and will use nest boxes as sleeping spots if roosts are lower
  • Nest boxes: one box per 3–4 hens, positioned lower than roosts
  • Raise the shed off the ground on concrete blocks or landscape blocks to prevent rodents nesting underneath and ground moisture rotting any wood floor boards
  • Add rubber stall mats to the floor for insulation and easy cleaning

Predator Proofing

  • Add a pop door (small chicken entrance) — cut a small opening in the shed wall or door panel, frame it, and add a sliding door or auto-door
  • All vent and window openings covered with ½" hardware cloth, secured with screws and washers — not staples
  • Lock the main door with a proper latch; raccoons can open simple slide latches
  • If raising the shed on blocks, wrap the gap with hardware cloth apron buried 6–12" underground to deter diggers
Space Rule of Thumb 4 sq ft per bird inside the coop, 10 sq ft per bird in the run. A common 7×7 Rubbermaid shed gives ~49 sq ft — comfortable for 10–12 birds with a run attached.
Zone 6b Winter Note Plastic sheds have less insulation than wood. In Zone 6b, the coop will get cold — but healthy adult chickens handle cold much better than poor ventilation. Keep airflow going even in winter; just avoid direct drafts on the roosts. Don't use a heat lamp unless you have chicks or sick birds — fire risk is significant with heat lamps in enclosed spaces.
