Feed

DIY Fodder System

Hydroponic sprouted grain for livestock. 5 lb of seed becomes 25 lb of highly digestible fresh feed in 6–8 days.

Overview

What Is Fodder?

Fodder is sprouted seeds — usually barley or wheat — grown hydroponically (no soil). The entire sprout including the seed and root mat is fed to animals. The sprouting process dramatically increases digestibility and nutrient availability compared to dry grain.

FactorDetail
Seed-to-feed ratio5 lb seed → ~25 lb fodder
Days to harvest6–8 days
DigestibilityNearly 100%
Best seedsClean barley or wheat
Feed rate~1–2% of animal body weight per day
Important Note Fodder is a supplement, not a complete feed. Works best alongside regular dry feed. Needs climate control in extreme temperatures.

Setup

Materials Needed

Equipment

  • Clean barley or wheat seed
  • Plastic trays with drain holes
  • Racks or PVC shelving to hold trays
  • Buckets for soaking
  • Water source for daily rinsing
  • Optional: diluted bleach solution for sanitizing trays between batches

Daily Process

Step-by-Step Growing

  1. Day 0 — Soaking. Soak seeds in clean water for 12–24 hours. Drain well before moving to trays.
  2. Days 1–2 — Sprouting. Spread soaked seeds in trays in a single layer. Place in a ventilated area with some light. Drain excess water daily.
  3. Days 3–5 — Growing. Rinse and mist seeds twice daily. Green blades begin to emerge. Maintain good air circulation to prevent mold.
  4. Day 6 or 7 — Harvest. Fodder should be 3–6" tall. The entire mat — green blades, roots, and seed — is pulled out of the tray and fed directly to livestock.
Daily Rotation Start a new tray every day so one batch is ready each day. With a 7-day cycle you need 7 trays running at staggered stages simultaneously.

Maintenance

Feeding & Upkeep

Feeding Guidelines

Feed approximately 1–2% of the animal's body weight per day in fodder. This works for chickens, rabbits, goats, cattle, and most other livestock.

Tray Care

Rinse trays after each use. Sanitize periodically with diluted bleach solution (rinse thoroughly after sanitizing). Reuse rinse water for garden irrigation if it's clean and uncontaminated.

Watch For Mold is the main enemy — it usually means too little airflow, too much water, or seeds spread too thick. If mold appears, sanitize the tray and reduce seed density on the next batch.
