Soil & Waste

Worm Composting

Turn kitchen scraps into nutrient-rich fertilizer with red wiggler worms. About 30 minutes a week, no smell when managed right.

Overview

What Is Vermicomposting?

Worm composting (also called vermicomposting) uses red wiggler worms to convert kitchen scraps into worm castings — a nutrient-dense fertilizer — and worm tea, a liquid fertilizer that drains from the bin. A DIY bin costs around $30 to set up. Even a store-bought system rarely exceeds $150 and can pay for itself in one growing season compared to buying organic fertilizer.

Setup

Building or Choosing a Bin

A worm bin needs three things: ventilation, drainage, and darkness. That's it.

DIY Indoor Bin (Recommended Starting Point)

A plastic storage tote works well. Drill ventilation holes in the sides every few inches. Drill drainage holes in the bottom — not the lid. Worms don't need light so the lid stays solid. Elevate the bin on small plastic containers (like cream cheese or sour cream lids glued upside down) so drainage can escape and air can circulate underneath. Set a second, slightly larger tray underneath to catch any drip.

Avoid the Double-Tote Design Nesting one tote inside a second identical tote and sealing the outer lid blocks the ventilation holes and traps too much moisture. The EPA recommends a shallow catch tray underneath instead, with the inner bin elevated.

DIY Outdoor Bin

A simple wood box with gaps between planks (like a pallet) works well outdoors. Line with heavy-duty greenhouse plastic punctured with drainage and airflow holes. Raise it off the ground the same way as the indoor bin.

Tiered / Multi-Tray Systems

Commercial systems like the Worm Factory 360 use stacking trays. You start in the bottom tray; once it's full, add a second tray on top. Worms migrate upward to the fresh food, leaving finished castings in the bottom tray for easy harvest. These eliminate the need to separate worms from finished compost by hand — a big time saver once your bin is producing well.

DIY Bin Materials

  • Plastic storage tote — shallow is better than deep (worms work near the surface)
  • Drill + bit (~¼" holes in sides and bottom)
  • Catch tray (second shallow tote or drip tray)
  • Small plastic containers to elevate the bin (glued upside down to the bottom)
  • Lid — solid, no holes needed

Getting Started

Bedding

Worms need a moist, carbon-rich bedding to live in. Good bedding materials:

Good Bedding

  • Shredded newspaper or cardboard (tear or shred into strips)
  • Egg cartons torn up
  • Leaves or straw — outdoors only; these can introduce insects if used indoors
  • A scoop of garden soil (adds beneficial microbes)

After setting up the bedding, add water until it feels like a wrung-out sponge — damp but not dripping. This is the right moisture level to maintain throughout. Don't add worms until the moisture level is right. Getting it wrong before adding worms is much easier to fix than trying to correct it after, since food scraps raise moisture significantly.

If the Bedding Gets Too Wet Add more dry shredded paper or cardboard. Do not add more food scraps until it dries out a bit — food adds moisture. If you accidentally added worms to a too-wet bin, add a thick layer of dry bedding on top and stop feeding temporarily.

How Much and How Often

The key mistake is overfeeding. Worms eat up to half their body weight per day under ideal conditions, but a new bin with a less established worm population needs time to build up. Start by burying small amounts of food in one corner of the bin. Rotate corners each feeding so worms work through the whole bin evenly. Only add more food once the previous batch has mostly disappeared.

Chop or Freeze Scraps First Smaller pieces break down faster. Freezing scraps before adding them breaks down cell walls and speeds processing significantly — worms get to the food more easily. If you freeze your kitchen scraps throughout the week and add a thawed batch once or twice a week, the bin stays tidier and more manageable.

Feed at a roughly 50/50 ratio of food scraps to carbon material (shredded paper, dry cardboard). This keeps moisture balanced and prevents the bin from becoming acidic or smelly.

Output

Harvesting Castings & Worm Tea

Worm Castings

Harvest every 3–6 months, or when the bin looks more like dark earthy compost than bedding and food scraps. Two harvesting methods:

  1. Migration method (easiest). Stop adding food to one side of the bin for 2–3 weeks and add all new food to the other side. Worms migrate toward the food. After a few weeks, the food-free side will be mostly castings with few worms — scoop it out.
  2. Dump and sort. Dump the entire bin onto a tarp in bright light. Worms hate light and will ball up in the center. Scrape off the outer layers of castings, then repeat as worms ball tighter. Time-consuming but thorough.

Using Castings

Use as a top dressing around plants, mix into potting soil at a 10–20% ratio, or work into garden beds before planting. Castings improve water retention, suppress some plant diseases, and make nutrients available in a form plants absorb easily. A little goes a long way — it's concentrated.

Worm Tea

The liquid that drains from the bin is concentrated fertilizer. Always dilute before use — a ratio of 1 part worm tea to 10 parts water is the standard recommendation. Apply diluted tea to soil around plants or use as a foliar spray. Harvest worm tea more frequently than castings; it accumulates in the catch tray and can go stagnant if left too long.

Don't Waste the Liquid Even if you're not gardening actively, diluted worm tea is excellent for houseplants or can be poured around trees and shrubs. It shouldn't go down the drain — it's too good for that.

Troubleshooting

Common Problems & Fixes

ProblemLikely CauseFix
Bad smell Overfeeding, too much moisture, or meat/dairy added Stop feeding temporarily; add dry shredded paper; remove any meat/dairy; bury food more deeply
Fruit flies Food left on surface, too much fruit, overfeeding Bury all food scraps under bedding; add a dry layer of paper on top; reduce fruit temporarily
Bin too wet Too many high-moisture scraps; not enough carbon Add shredded newspaper or cardboard; slow down feeding wet scraps
Bin too dry Not enough moisture in scraps; low-humidity environment Mist with a spray bottle; add wetter scraps; check worms aren't in distress
Worms escaping Bin too wet, too acidic, or newly set up (they explore) Check moisture and pH; add crushed eggshells to neutralize acidity; put a light source near the bin at night temporarily to discourage wandering
Worms dying Temperature extremes, toxic food, bin too acidic, or bin too wet (drowning) Move bin to stable 55–77°F location; check for prohibited foods; add crushed eggshells; improve drainage
Slow breakdown Too cold; food pieces too large; bin too dry; not enough worms Warm the bin; chop scraps smaller or freeze before adding; check moisture; consider adding more worms
Eggshells Are Your Friend Crushed eggshells help neutralize acidity in the bin (worm castings are naturally acidic over time), provide grit that aids worm digestion, and add calcium. Add them regularly — they're free from your kitchen and solve several problems at once.

Paterstead Integration

Closing the Loop

A worm bin fits naturally into a homestead system because almost everything connects:

How It Connects to Other Projects

  • Kitchen scraps → worm bin → worm castings → garden / raised beds
  • Worm tea (diluted 1:10) → liquid fertilizer for aquaponics grow beds or hydroponics (use cautiously — test first)
  • Excess worms → feed to chickens as a protein supplement
  • Spent mealworm frass (from the mealworm farm) → excellent worm bin addition; high nitrogen
  • Cardboard and paper waste from the household → bedding; keeps the bin balanced
  • Rabbit manure → one of the best worm bin amendments; "cold" manure that won't burn worms and is high in nutrients
Rabbit Manure Is Special Unlike chicken or pig manure, rabbit manure is a "cold" manure — it doesn't need to be composted before use and won't burn plants or harm worms. Adding it directly to the worm bin accelerates breakdown and significantly boosts the nutrient content of your castings.
