Overview

How Aquaponics Works

Aquaponics combines fish farming (aquaculture) with soilless plant growing (hydroponics) in a single recirculating system. Fish produce waste that breaks down into ammonia. Beneficial bacteria in the grow bed media convert that ammonia into nitrites, then into nitrates — which are plant fertilizer. The plants absorb the nitrates, cleaning the water, which flows back to the fish. Everyone wins.

ComponentRole
FishProduce waste that becomes plant fertilizer
Bacteria (in grow media)Convert ammonia → nitrites → nitrates
PlantsAbsorb nitrates, filter and clean the water
PumpCirculates water from fish tank to grow bed and back
Bell siphonAutomates the flood-and-drain cycle without a timer
Grow mediaSupports plant roots and hosts the bacteria colony
Aquaponics vs. Hydroponics In hydroponics you add synthetic fertilizer to the water manually. In aquaponics, the fish do that work for you — and you get fish as a second food crop. The tradeoff is more complexity: you're managing a living ecosystem, not just plants and water.

System Design

System Types

Media-Bed (Flood and Drain) — Best for Beginners

Grow beds are filled with media (expanded clay, lava rock, gravel) and flood periodically with water pumped from the fish tank, then drain back. The media hosts the bacteria colony and supports plant roots. This is the most beginner-friendly design and what both reference videos use. The flood-and-drain cycle is controlled automatically by a bell siphon — no timer needed.

Deep Water Culture (Raft)

Plants float on rafts above a large trough of water with roots submerged. Needs a separate mechanical filter to remove solids before water reaches the plants (the media bed handles this automatically in flood-and-drain). Common in commercial operations.

Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)

A thin stream of water flows through angled channels past plant roots. Requires the most careful management and is least forgiving for beginners — roots dry out fast if the pump fails.

Build Option 1 · Beginner · ~$200–300

IBC Chop & Flip System

From the Hoocho video. A single IBC (Intermediate Bulk Container) tote is cut and flipped to create both the fish tank and the grow bed in one compact unit. One of the most popular starter builds — it's a one-day project and can be expanded later.

What You Need

  • 1 × food-grade IBC tote (275 or 330 gallon) — used ones run $100–200
  • Angle grinder or jigsaw to cut the IBC
  • Water pump (200–400 GPH rated for your tank volume)
  • Bell siphon kit (or build your own from PVC)
  • Bulkhead fittings for drain and plumbing
  • Vinyl tubing and PVC fittings
  • Grow media: expanded clay pebbles (hydroton/LECA) or lava rock — fill grow bed to ~1" below rim
  • Air pump + airstone (for fish tank oxygenation)
  • Water test kit (ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, pH)

How to Build It

  1. Strip down the IBC. Remove the plastic cage from around the tote (or cut it down). The steel cage frame will be reused as structural support.
  2. Mark and cut. Measure and mark a cut line about 12" down from the top of the tote. Cut all the way around. You now have two pieces: the large bottom section (fish tank) and the smaller top section (grow bed).
  3. Flip the top section. Invert the cut-off top so the cut edge faces up. This creates a shallow tray — your grow bed — that sits on top of the fish tank using the cage frame as a stand.
  4. Install the drain fitting. Drill a hole in the lowest point of the grow bed (the original top of the IBC) and install a bulkhead fitting. This is where the bell siphon will drain back to the fish tank.
  5. Install the bell siphon. The bell siphon automates flooding and draining. Water fills the grow bed; when it reaches the top of the standpipe, the siphon fires and rapidly drains the bed; the vacuum breaks when the bed is nearly empty, and the cycle repeats. No timer needed — runs continuously while the pump runs.
  6. Install the pump. Place the water pump in the fish tank. Run tubing up and into the grow bed. The pump pushes water up; the bell siphon drains it back down.
  7. Add aeration. Run airline tubing from an air pump to an airstone in the fish tank. Fish need dissolved oxygen — this is not optional.
  8. Fill with media. Fill the grow bed with expanded clay pebbles or lava rock to about 1" below the rim. Rinse media first.
  9. Cycle the system before adding fish. See the cycling section below.
Wood Cladding Tip (from the video) Wrapping the IBC frame with pallet wood looks much better, helps block sunlight (reducing algae growth in the fish tank), and insulates the water temperature. Worth doing before you fill it.
Footprint A standard 275-gallon IBC chop-and-flip fits in roughly a 48" × 40" area. Very manageable for a backyard or covered patio.

Build Option 2 · Experienced · Scale Up

Multi-Bed IBC System

From the Shawn Paul / MorningStar Aquaponics video. This is a larger backyard system using two fish tanks, six grow beds, and biofilters — built from IBC tanks cut in half. The video went viral in the aquaponics community and is a great reference for understanding how a more complete system is laid out.

Key Design Features

The tanks are elevated on concrete blocks so all plumbing runs underneath. Every water outlet has a spigot for manual flow control. Each grow bed drains via a bell siphon. The system runs two large fish tanks with three grow beds per tank.

Scaling Up from the Chop & Flip The IBC chop-and-flip is designed to be expandable. Once you're comfortable with the basics, you add more fish tanks and grow beds using the same flood-and-drain principles. The Shawn Paul video shows what a mature expanded system looks like.

What's Different at This Scale

  • Multiple IBCs cut in half to create separate fish tanks and grow beds
  • Dedicated biofilters to support heavier fish loads
  • Plumbing runs underneath tanks for a clean setup
  • Manual spigots on each outlet for fine-tuned flow control
  • One bell siphon per grow bed
  • System still uses a single pump (CHOP-style — Constant Height One Pump)

Key Component

Bell Siphons

The bell siphon is the heart of a flood-and-drain aquaponics system. It creates an automatic flooding and draining cycle using only gravity and water pressure — no timers, no electricity in the siphon itself.

How It Works (4-Phase Cycle)

  1. Fill. The pump pushes water into the grow bed. The water level slowly rises through the media.
  2. Siphon fires. When water reaches the top of the standpipe inside the bell, it begins overflowing down through the pipe. This triggers a vacuum (siphon) effect that rapidly pulls all remaining water out of the bed.
  3. Drain. The grow bed empties quickly — roots get their air exposure during this phase, which is critical for plant health and bacteria activity.
  4. Break. When the bed is nearly empty, air enters through slits at the base of the bell and breaks the siphon. The pump immediately starts filling the bed again and the cycle repeats.
Media Guard Always install a media guard (a perforated tube) around the bell siphon to keep clay pebbles, roots, and debris from clogging it. This is the most common cause of siphon failure.
Bell Siphon Troubleshooting If the siphon won't fire, the pump flow rate is probably too slow — increase flow. If the siphon won't break (keeps draining), the flow rate is too fast — reduce it or adjust the standpipe height. Getting the bell siphon dialed in is the trickiest part of the build; plan to spend time on it.

Critical First Step

Cycling the System

Cycling is the process of building up a healthy colony of nitrifying bacteria in your grow media before adding fish or plants. This is the most important step beginners skip — adding fish to an uncycled system will kill them from ammonia buildup.

The bacteria you need live in the grow media and convert: ammonia → nitrites → nitrates. This process takes 4–6 weeks. You can cycle with or without fish.

Fishless Cycling (Recommended for Beginners)

  1. Fill the system with dechlorinated water and run the pump.
  2. Add an ammonia source — pure ammonia (no surfactants), a small amount of fish food that rots, or urine. Target 2–4 ppm ammonia.
  3. Test water daily with an aquarium test kit. Watch for ammonia to rise, then nitrites to rise (as bacteria start converting), then nitrates to appear (as the second bacteria type establishes).
  4. The system is cycled when ammonia and nitrites both read 0 ppm and nitrates are climbing. This usually takes 4–6 weeks.
  5. Do a large water change to bring nitrates below 40 ppm, then add fish.
Do Not Skip This Ammonia and nitrites are toxic to fish at low concentrations. An uncycled system will kill fish within days. There are no shortcuts — the bacteria need time to establish.

Fish Selection

Choosing Your Fish

FishBest ForZone 6b Notes
Goldfish / Koi Absolute beginners; ornamental Hardy; can overwinter outdoors in insulated tanks or indoors
Tilapia Edible fish; fast growing; easy to raise Tropical — need water above 60°F; system must come indoors in winter or be heated
Trout Edible fish; cool-water species Good for Zone 6b — prefer 50–65°F water; ideal for spring/fall outdoor growing
Catfish Edible; tolerates lower oxygen and water quality swings More tolerant than tilapia; still needs temps above 55°F
Perch Edible; cold-tolerant Good option for cooler climates; slower growing than tilapia
Stocking Rate A general starting rule is 1 lb of fish per 5–10 gallons of water. Overloading the fish tank before your bacteria colony is mature enough to handle the waste is a common beginner mistake. Start with fewer fish and add more as the system stabilizes.
Zone 6b Winter Consideration If you want to raise edible warm-water fish like tilapia, plan to either move the system indoors in October or add a water heater. Trout or perch are easier choices for an outdoor system since they handle cold water naturally.

Media

Grow Bed Media

The media in your grow bed does three jobs: supports plant roots, hosts the bacteria colony, and filters solids from the water. Choose media that is pH-neutral and won't leach chemicals into the water.

MediaProsCons
Expanded clay (hydroton/LECA) pH neutral; reusable; great drainage and airflow; easy to work with More expensive; limited water retention
Lava rock Cheap; excellent surface area for bacteria; pH neutral Sharp edges can damage roots; heavy; harder to source in uniform size
Gravel Inexpensive; widely available Must test pH — some gravel raises it; less surface area for bacteria; heavy
Do Not Use Perlite in Aquaponics Perlite can be harmful to fish — it was flagged specifically in the UMN Extension hydroponics guide for this reason. Stick to clay pebbles, lava rock, or tested gravel for aquaponics.
Media Depth Aim for 12" of media depth in your grow bed. Bell siphons are typically designed around this depth. Shallower beds may not give roots enough room or provide sufficient bacterial surface area.

Maintenance

Water Quality

You'll need an aquarium test kit that measures at minimum: ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH. Test weekly once the system is established, daily during cycling or if fish look stressed.

ParameterTarget RangeNotes
pH6.8 – 7.2Compromise between fish (prefer 7–8) and plants (prefer 6–7). Test frequently.
Ammonia (NH3)0 ppmAny reading above 0.5 ppm is dangerous to fish. Source: fish waste + uneaten food.
Nitrite (NO2)0 ppmToxic to fish; produced by bacteria during cycling. Should stay at 0 in a cycled system.
Nitrate (NO3)Below 40 ppmPlant fertilizer — some is good. Very high levels stress fish. Plants keep it in check.
Dissolved oxygenAbove 6 mg/LCritical for fish and bacteria. Run an airstone; never let the pump stop for long.
Water tempDepends on fish speciesTilapia: 70–85°F. Trout: 50–65°F. Goldfish: 50–75°F.

Top-Off and Water Changes

Unlike hydroponics, you rarely do full water changes in aquaponics — that would destroy your bacterial colony. Instead, top off with dechlorinated water as the level drops from evaporation and plant uptake. Let tap water sit 24 hours to dechlorinate, or use a dechlorinator product.

Never Use Chlorinated Tap Water Directly Chlorine kills the beneficial bacteria your entire system depends on. Always dechlorinate first, either by letting water sit 24 hours or using sodium thiosulfate drops.

What to Grow

Plants for Aquaponics

Most plants that grow in hydroponics also do well in aquaponics. Leafy greens and herbs are the easiest and most productive. Fruiting plants like tomatoes and peppers need higher nutrient levels — only attempt them once your fish load is high enough to support it.

CategoryGood ChoicesNotes
EasiestLettuce, kale, Swiss chard, spinach, basil, mint, parsleyLow nutrient demand; fast-growing; ideal for new systems
IntermediateBeans, peas, cucumbers, pak choi, beetsHigher nutrient demand; do well once system is established
AdvancedTomatoes, peppers, strawberriesNeed higher nitrate levels; require more fish to support
AvoidRoot vegetables (carrots, potatoes)Need deep soil; don't work in media-bed systems

Quick Reference

System Checklist

Before You Add Fish

  • System is fully assembled and plumbing is leak-free
  • Bell siphon is cycling reliably (fill → drain → break → repeat)
  • Grow bed media is rinsed and in place
  • Air pump and airstone are running in fish tank
  • System has been cycled (ammonia and nitrites both read 0 ppm)
  • Water is dechlorinated and pH is 6.8–7.2
  • You have a test kit on hand

Ongoing Weekly Tasks

  • Test ammonia, nitrite, nitrate, and pH
  • Check that pump and siphon are cycling correctly
  • Top off with dechlorinated water as needed
  • Remove dead plant material promptly
  • Check fish for signs of stress or disease
  • Harvest plants and replant to keep nutrient uptake active
