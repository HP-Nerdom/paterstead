Growing

Small-Scale Hydroponics

Grow greens, herbs, and vegetables year-round — indoors or out — without soil.

Overview

Why Hydroponics

Hydroponics is growing plants in water rather than soil. It's space-efficient, uses significantly less water than soil gardening, and produces no weeds. With artificial lighting you can grow year-round — which makes it especially valuable in Zone 6b where outdoor growing seasons are limited.

FactorDetail
Best year-round cropsLettuce, herbs, kale, mustard greens, swiss chard, amaranth
Best summer outdoor cropsTomatoes, cucumbers, strawberries, peppers
Water useLess than soil-based gardening
WeedsNone
Growth rateFaster than soil with proper nutrients and light
Zone 6b Note Outdoor hydroponic growing works great from late spring through early fall. For winter growing, bring the system indoors and add supplemental LED lighting. Leafy greens and herbs are the most practical year-round indoor crops.

System Types

Choosing a System

Deep Water Culture (DWC) — Best for Beginners

Plants are suspended above a tank of water with roots hanging down into the nutrient solution. This is the simplest, cheapest, and easiest system to maintain and expand. A 5-gallon bucket or plastic storage bin is all you need to start.

Kratky Method (Passive DWC) The simplest possible version — no pump needed. The air gap between the water surface and the net pot provides oxygen. Best for lettuce and herbs in a single container. Just start with the roots touching the water and let the level drop naturally as the plant grows.

Nutrient Film Technique (NFT)

Plants grow in angled channels or troughs. A pump continuously flows a thin stream of nutrient solution over the roots, which drains back into a reservoir. Efficient for larger setups because you manage one central reservoir. Can be built from recycled gutters. Requires a pump — if the pump fails, plants dry out fast.

Ebb and Flow

Plants grow in a tray with substrate. A pump periodically floods the tray with nutrient solution, which then drains back into the reservoir. Available as prefab units or DIY with buckets and basic plumbing supplies.

Drip Systems

Similar to NFT but water flows through a hose with holes spaced at each plant rather than an open channel. Increasingly common in high tunnels for tomatoes and strawberries grown in substrate-filled bags.

Pump-Dependent Systems NFT, ebb and flow, and drip systems all rely on pumps. A power outage can kill plants quickly since roots dry out fast. For a small homestead system, passive DWC (Kratky) or basic aerated DWC avoids this vulnerability.

Setup

Containers, Pots & Substrate

Container

Any food-safe container that holds water works — a 5-gallon bucket, a plastic storage tote, or a purpose-built hydroponic bin. Size should match the mature plant size (check the seed packet). Use a lid or a sheet of extruded polystyrene (foam insulation board) to support net pots over the water. If floating polystyrene directly on the water, add PVC tubes underneath for support as plants get heavier.

Net Pots

Pots with slotted sides that let roots reach the nutrient solution. The pot should only be partially submerged — roots need oxygen in the air above the water. Drill net-pot-sized holes in your lid so the lip of each pot rests on top.

Substrate (what goes in the net pots)

No soil. Instead, use one of the following. Soak any substrate in water for 24 hours and change the water before transplanting to remove debris and pH-affecting residues.

SubstrateProsCons
Rockwool Most popular; many sizes; retains oxygen well Not biodegradable; can irritate skin/lungs; alkaline (affects pH)
Coconut coir Good water retention; eco-friendly; reusable Variable quality; bricks need rehydrating; can harbor insects
Hydroton / LECA Great airflow; reusable; easy to clean Limited water retention; more expensive
Perlite Holds oxygen well; inexpensive; reusable Can float; small particles clog; not safe for aquaponics
Pumice Lightweight; various sizes; no super-heating needed Hard to find untreated; sharp edges can damage roots; can float
Gravel Inexpensive; drains well; easy to clean Heavy; can affect pH; small particles can clog

Lighting

Grow Lights

Outdoors in summer, natural sunlight is all you need. For indoor growing, supplemental lighting is almost always necessary — even a south-facing window usually isn't enough in winter in Zone 6b.

LED vs. Fluorescent

TypeProsCons
LED Most energy-efficient; long-lasting (~25,000 hrs); low heat; wide spectrum options Higher upfront cost
Fluorescent Cheaper upfront; moderately efficient Shorter lifespan; uses more energy than LED; check spectrum on label
Incandescent Cheapest upfront Inefficient; short lifespan — not recommended
High Pressure Sodium Emits substantial light Older technology; releases a lot of heat; better suited to large-scale

Red vs. Blue Light

For leafy greens only, blue light alone is fine. For flowering or fruiting plants (tomatoes, peppers, strawberries), you need red light too — it helps develop thicker stems. Look for bulbs labeled "full spectrum" or "white light" which produce a balance of both. As a bonus: less blue light produces milder, flatter lettuce; more blue light produces spicier, curlier lettuce.

Cost Example (from UMN Extension) A single 9W LED grow light for 1–2 five-gallon buckets costs about $25 upfront and roughly $0.45/month in electricity to run 14 hours/day. LEDs last ~5 years at that rate.

Aeration

Keeping Roots Oxygenated

Passive (Kratky Method)

The air gap between the water surface and the bottom of the net pot provides oxygen. Keep roots only 1/3 to 1/2 submerged once they develop past the net pot. No pump or airstone needed.

Active — Airstone

An airstone (the same porous stone used in fish tanks) connects to an external pump via tubing and releases oxygen as fine bubbles. Good for a single container with multiple plants, or when plants have different root lengths and some roots might end up too deeply submerged.

Active — Recirculating DWC

Multiple containers connected with tubing. A large pump circulates oxygenated water between containers and a central reservoir. You manage pH and nutrients in one place. Premade systems are available but pricey — good DIY tutorials exist on YouTube.

Water & Nutrients

Water Quality & Fertilizing

pH

Target pH is 5.4–7.0. Outside this range, plants can't efficiently absorb nutrients even if they're present. Test with pH strips (a 15-foot roll costs ~$7 and lasts years). Test when you first mix your nutrient solution and every few weeks during a crop cycle.

Adjusting pH

  • Too low (too acidic): add baking soda or a commercial pH-up product
  • Too high (too basic): add citric acid, phosphoric acid, or a commercial pH-down product
  • Hard water tends to be alkaline — if your faucets build up crust, assume alkaline water
  • Fix: use reverse osmosis water (available at most grocery stores, ~$0.39/gal refill)

Fertilizer

In soil, the ground provides most trace minerals. In hydroponics you supply everything: nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium, calcium, magnesium, sulfur, manganese, iron, and more. The simplest solution is a pre-made hydroponic fertilizer formulated for your specific crop (lettuce formula, tomato formula, etc.).

Liquid fertilizers are easy to measure but heavy and expensive to ship. Dry fertilizers are cheaper and lighter but sometimes come as multi-part mixes that you combine on-site. Either works — read the label for the recommended amount per gallon and use that concentration consistently when you top off water.

Keep crops with different needs separate Tomatoes and lettuce have different fertilizer requirements — don't mix them in the same container. Lettuce and kale can share a container if they need the same concentration.

Changing Water

For fast crops like lettuce (harvested in 6–7 weeks), you can top off without a full change. For longer crops like tomatoes, do a complete change-out once or twice per season to prevent algal and bacterial buildup. When doing a full change: dump the old water into the garden (it's fertilizer), scrub the container with dish soap, rinse well, and refill with fresh nutrient solution.

Planting

Starting Seeds & Transplanting

Start seeds separately and transplant into the hydroponic system after germination — don't direct-sow into net pots.

Option 1 — Starter Cubes

Rockwool or coconut coir starter cubes are available online or at garden stores. Soak the cube in water before use. Keep it partially submerged (not fully). When roots push through the bottom of the cube, it's ready to transplant directly into the net pot.

Option 2 — Potting Soil

Start seeds the normal way in potting soil. When transplanting into the hydroponic system, rinse the roots thoroughly to remove all soil — leftover soil can introduce pathogens and cloud the water.

Germination Environment Seeds need warmth and humidity to germinate. Use a container with a lid (plastic tote works well) near a south-facing window. A heating mat helps if you don't have good window light. Grow lights also work for starting seeds.

Maintenance

Pests, Disease & Sanitation

Insects

Indoors, the most common pests are small soft-bodied insects: aphids, thrips, mites, and whiteflies. They thrive indoors because there are no natural predators. Once established they multiply fast.

Insect Prevention

  • Don't bring outdoor plants in — they may carry pests
  • Inspect plants from garden stores before bringing home
  • Use fresh substrate and clean water for every new batch
  • Clean and sanitize all containers between plantings
  • Keep the hydroponic setup away from other houseplants
  • Scout regularly — check undersides of leaves; use yellow sticky traps

For minor infestations: remove and isolate the affected plant. For aphids, mites, or mealybugs, dab leaves with diluted dish soap on a cotton ball. If infestation becomes serious, harvest everything, clean the system, and start fresh to break the life cycle.

Disease

Most common problems: powdery mildew, downy mildew, and root rot (Pythium). Prevention is the best approach — treatment is difficult once disease takes hold.

Disease Prevention

  • Start with disease-free seed and clean materials
  • Wash hands before working with the system
  • Keep growing area above 65°F, especially during germination
  • Don't crowd plants — follow seed packet spacing
  • Add a small fan for airflow
  • Never fully submerge roots — they need the oxygen above the waterline
  • Remove diseased plants immediately

Cleaning Between Plantings

  1. Drain the water (pour into garden — it's fertilizer).
  2. Scrub all surfaces with dish soap and warm water using a scrub brush.
  3. Rinse with clean water.
  4. Sanitize: spray or soak in a bleach solution (1 tablespoon household bleach per 1 gallon water). Let air dry — do not rinse after sanitizing.
  5. Clean net pots, polystyrene boards, and any other components the same way.

Harvest

Harvesting

Continuous Harvest (herbs, leaf lettuce)

Cut the most mature (lowest) leaves or stems as needed and let the plant keep growing. Most hydroponic growers harvest 3–5 times from the same plants before starting fresh with new seed. Growth slows over time — that's the signal to replant.

Single Harvest (head lettuce)

Head lettuce varieties are harvested once and won't resprout. Cut at the base and start a new seedling.

Food Safety at Harvest

Harvest with clean hands using clean tools (scissors or knife). Rinse all produce thoroughly before eating. When pulling root-attached plants out of the system, minimize splashing so nutrient water doesn't get on edible parts of other plants. Hydroponic systems are warm, nutrient-rich environments — bacteria can reproduce quickly if introduced.
