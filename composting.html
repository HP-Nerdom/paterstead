Composting

DIY bin builds, composting methods, and what goes in — or doesn't. For worm-specific composting, see the Worm Bin page.

Overview

Composting Methods at a Glance

The right method depends on what you're composting, how fast you want results, and how much space and effort you want to put in. All of them produce the same end product — but on very different timelines.

MethodSpeedEffortBest For
Cold composting 6–18 months Low — pile it and leave it Yard waste, garden scraps, large volumes
Hot composting 4–8 weeks High — turn frequently, monitor temp Kitchen + yard waste, fast turnaround
Tumbler 4–8 weeks Medium — spin every few days Pest-free, neat, small to medium volume
Trench composting 1–3 months Low once dug Garden beds, no bin needed
Bokashi 2–4 weeks to pre-compost Low — sealed bucket system Meat, dairy, cooked food — stuff other methods can't handle
Vermicomposting 3–6 months for castings Low — ~30 min/week Kitchen scraps, indoor use, apartment-friendly
Zone 6b Timing Outdoor composting slows dramatically below 50°F and essentially stops when frozen. In Zone 6b, hot composting works well April through October. Cold piles just pause in winter and resume in spring. Move your worm bin indoors before first frost (mid-October).

Browns, Greens & What to Avoid

Composting runs on the ratio of carbon-rich "browns" to nitrogen-rich "greens." Get this balance right and your pile decomposes quickly without smelling bad.

Browns (Carbon)Greens (Nitrogen)Never Compost
Dry leaves Grass clippings Meat, fish, bones
Shredded newspaper / cardboard Fruit and vegetable scraps Dairy products
Straw or hay Coffee grounds and tea bags Fats and oils
Sawdust (untreated) Fresh plant clippings Pet or human waste
Wood chips or twigs Eggshells Diseased plants
Paper bags, egg cartons Hair and nail clippings Chemically treated wood

The Ratio

For cold composting, just make sure you have more browns than greens — the exact ratio isn't critical. For hot composting (aiming for 130–160°F to kill weed seeds and pathogens), aim for 2–4 parts browns to 1 part greens by volume. Too many greens = smelly, slimy pile. Too many browns = pile barely decomposes.

How to Layer

  1. Start with a browns layer. A 4–6" base of coarse carbon material (wood chips, straw, dry leaves) creates airflow at the bottom.
  2. Add a greens layer. Kitchen scraps, fresh clippings, coffee grounds — 2–3" deep. Bury food scraps rather than leaving them on top to deter pests and flies.
  3. Cover with more browns. Always end with a carbon layer on top to control odor and moisture.
  4. Moisten if needed. The pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge throughout — damp but not dripping.
  5. Repeat. Keep layering as you generate material. Turn the pile periodically to add oxygen and speed things up.

DIY Builds

Bin Options — Choose Your Build

Source: Lawn Love — 13 Easy DIY Compost Bins

1. Plastic Trash Can Bin

Best for small spaces — a porch, garage, or cramped yard corner. Creates a closed environment that retains heat and moisture well, making it good for both hot and cold composting.

Materials

  • 32-gallon plastic trash can with a tight-fitting lid (use an old one — you'll be drilling it)
  • Drill with a 2–3" bit
  • Aluminum screen fabric (from an old window screen works fine)
  • Silicone caulk or epoxy adhesive
  1. Drill holes around the walls every 6–12" for ventilation.
  2. Cut 4–5" squares of screen fabric and glue one over each hole from the inside. This keeps pests out while letting air flow.
  3. Let the glue dry completely before adding material.

2. Pallet Bin

Free pallets are easy to find. This design is large enough for yard waste — grass clippings, dry leaves, twigs, branches. The open bottom lets the pile contact soil, which brings in beneficial microbes.

Materials

  • 4 similarly-sized wooden pallets (free from hardware stores, garden centers, restaurants)
  • 4 L-brackets and wood screws
  • 2 heavy-duty strap hinges (for the front door pallet)
  • Gate hook and latch + handle
  • Power drill, screwdriver, hammer
  1. Set one pallet as the back wall. Attach a second pallet to its right side at 90° using two L-brackets.
  2. Attach the third pallet to the left side the same way.
  3. Use strap hinges to hang the fourth pallet as a door on the front opening.
  4. Add a hook, latch, and handle to keep it closed and easy to open for turning.

3. Wire Fence Bin

Fastest and cheapest build. Ideal as a secondary bin for collecting garden scraps or for cold composting yard waste. Not ideal for hot composting since it offers no protection from weather.

Materials

  • ~10 feet of wire fencing (chicken wire, hardware cloth, or rabbit wire) for a 3-ft diameter circle
  • Wire cutters
  • Zip ties or wire
  • Work gloves
  1. Cut fencing to length. Lay flat and walk over it to flatten any curl.
  2. Form into a circle and fasten the ends together with zip ties at top, middle, and bottom.
  3. Place in your chosen spot — allow room around it to turn the pile with a fork.

4. Wood Slat Bin

The classic backyard compost bin. Sturdy, good moisture and heat retention, excellent for hot composting. The removable front slats make turning and harvesting easy.

Materials

  • Three 2×3 or 2×4 boards for vertical posts (3¼ ft tall)
  • Twenty-four 1×6 or 2×6 horizontal slats (3¼ ft long)
  • Saw, electric screwdriver, two C-clamps, screws
  • Use untreated wood only — treated lumber leaches chemicals into compost

Build two side panels (each with two vertical posts and slats with 1" gaps between slats for airflow), attach them with back slats, then slide removable front slats into grooves in the front posts. The sliding front lets you remove just enough to turn the pile or harvest finished compost from the bottom.

5. Compost Tumbler

A sealed barrel on an axle — spin it to turn the compost without a fork. Best perk: fully enclosed, so rodents and raccoons can't get in. Good for households that want fast results and hate manual turning.

Materials

  • 55-gallon plastic barrel with lid
  • Two 4×4 wood posts as a stand
  • Steel rod for the axle (runs through the center of the barrel)
  • Drill, saw, screws

Cut an access door in the barrel side (with hinges and a latch). Drill ventilation holes. Mount the steel rod through the center lengthwise. Cut notches in the tops of the two upright posts to cradle the rod. Mount the barrel so it spins freely. Fill through the door, spin every few days.

6. Bokashi Bin (For Meat & Dairy)

The only method that handles meat, dairy, cooked food, and fats — materials other composting methods can't take. Bokashi uses anaerobic fermentation (lacto-fermentation, like sauerkraut) rather than aerobic decomposition. The output is a pre-compost that breaks down very quickly when buried in soil or added to a regular compost pile.

Materials

  • Two identical food-grade buckets with lids (round or square both work)
  • Drill
  • Bokashi bran (inoculated wheat bran — the active ingredient, available online)
  1. Drill ~20 holes in the bottom of one bucket. Nest it inside the second (undrilled) bucket — liquid drains through the holes into the lower bucket.
  2. Add food waste in layers, pressing down firmly to remove air pockets each time.
  3. Sprinkle a thin layer of Bokashi bran over each food addition.
  4. Seal the lid tightly after every use. Bokashi is anaerobic — air stops the fermentation.
  5. Once the bucket is full, seal and let ferment 2 weeks. Then bury the pre-compost in the garden or add to a regular bin. Drain the liquid from the lower bucket weekly — dilute 1:100 with water and use as fertilizer, or pour undiluted down a drain to clean pipes.
Bokashi Pre-Compost Is Acidic The fermented output smells pickled (normal) and is too acidic to use directly on plants. It needs to be buried in soil or mixed into a regular compost pile where it finishes breaking down in 2–4 weeks. Don't apply it directly to plant roots.

7. Trench Composting

No bin at all — dig a trench, fill it with scraps, cover with soil. The simplest possible method. Great for garden beds since you're essentially fertilizing exactly where you need it.

  1. Dig a trench 12" deep in or between garden rows.
  2. Add food scraps and any brown carbon material.
  3. Lightly water the waste.
  4. Cover with at least 8" of soil to deter animals and control odor.
  5. Plant over or beside it the following season — the buried material will have broken down.

Worm Bin · Official Guide

EPA Indoor Worm Bin Setup

This is the EPA's recommended two-bin worm composting design — the one referenced in the Eartheasy article as the best DIY approach. Simple, well-ventilated, easy to maintain. Full guide: EPA.gov | Also see the full Worm Composting page.

What You Need

  • Two plastic bins — one taller (inner bin, ~18 gal, 15"D × 20"W × 15"H), one shorter and wider (outer/catch tray, ~5"H — just needs to be wider than the inner bin)
  • Drill with a 1" bit and a ⅛" bit
  • Vinyl window screening — four 4"×4" scraps (do not use metal screen — it rusts)
  • Waterproof glue
  • Shredded paper (enough to fill bin 3" deep, plus extra for weekly use)
  • About 1 lb of garden soil
  • Water
  • 1 lb of red wigglers (Eisenia fetida)
  • Trowel
  • Small lidded container for collecting kitchen scraps during the week

Why the Two-Bin Design

The tall inner bin holds the worms and compost. The short outer bin catches worm tea (liquid fertilizer) that drains through holes in the inner bin's bottom. The outer bin has no holes — it's just a catch tray. The inner bin sits elevated inside the outer one so drainage can actually flow.

Preparing the Bins

  1. Drill air holes. In the tall inner bin: drill two 1" holes near the top (one on each side) for airflow. Then drill four ⅛" holes near each bottom corner for drainage.
  2. Screen the holes. Cut vinyl screen squares and glue one over each hole from the inside. Let glue dry completely before proceeding — worms escape through any gap.
  3. Do not drill the outer bin. It's only a catch tray for worm tea.
  4. Place inner bin inside outer bin. If the inner bin sits flat on the outer bin's bottom, elevate it slightly (upside-down small containers work) so drainage can pool underneath without the inner bin sitting in it.

Setting Up Bedding and Adding Worms

  1. Shred paper into strips. Soak in water, then wring out so it's damp but not dripping — wet sponge consistency.
  2. Mix damp shredded paper with 1 lb of garden soil. Fill the inner bin about 3" deep with this mixture.
  3. Add red wigglers on top. Let them settle for a full day before feeding — don't rush it.

Feeding

Collect fruit and vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, and tea bags in your lidded container throughout the week. Feed the worms once a week — they do best left undisturbed between feedings.

  1. Use the trowel to create a small hole in the bedding.
  2. Place scraps in the hole.
  3. Cover all food completely with moist paper or soil. Exposed food attracts fruit flies; covered food does not.
  4. Rotate where you bury food each week so worms work through the whole bin evenly.
  5. Only add new food once previous scraps have mostly been consumed.
Never Feed These Meat, fish, bones, dairy, fat. Also remove any scraps the worms haven't touched after a week — they may not like that food. If pieces are large, chop them smaller next time.

Maintenance & Harvesting

Every few months, scoop the liquid from the outer (catch) bin and dilute 1:10 with water for use on plants. When the inner bin fills to the air holes, it's harvest time:

  1. Feed only one side of the bin for 2–3 weeks. Worms migrate toward the food.
  2. Harvest the castings from the unfed side — most worms will have moved away from it.
  3. Use harvested castings in pots, garden beds, or spread across the yard.

Quick Reference

Troubleshooting Outdoor Bins

ProblemCauseFix
Smells like ammonia Too many greens / nitrogen Add more browns; turn the pile
Smells like rotten eggs Too wet, not enough oxygen Turn the pile; add dry browns; improve drainage
Pile not heating up Too dry, too small, or too much carbon Add water and greens; ensure pile is at least 3 ft × 3 ft
Pests / rodents Food scraps exposed, or meat/dairy added Bury scraps; switch to a closed bin or tumbler; never add meat or dairy to an open bin
Pile not breaking down Too dry or too cold Add water; wait until spring if frozen; chop materials smaller
Flies Food scraps left exposed on surface Always bury scraps under a layer of browns
Finished Compost Looks Like Dark brown, crumbly, earthy-smelling material. You shouldn't be able to identify the original ingredients. If you can still see scraps or recognizable pieces, it needs more time. Sift out any large unfinished pieces and toss them back in for another round.
